Hassan Sheep

Hassan

Hassan sheep are a notable breed indigenous to the Hassan district of Karnataka, India, primarily raised for meat production. These sheep are well-adapted to the local environment and play a significant role in the livelihoods of small and marginal farmers in the region.

Adult male Hassan sheep have an average body length of 64.2 cm, a height at withers of 65.6 cm, and a chest girth of 74.3 cm, while females measure slightly smaller, with a body length of 62.8 cm, height at withers of 63.7 cm, and chest girth of 72.1 cm. The tail length averages 12.5 cm in males and 11.2 cm in females.

At birth, males weigh around 2.1 kg, while females weigh about 2.0 kg. By 3 months, males reach approximately 14.1 kg and females 13.4 kg. At 6 months, males weigh 19.0 kg and females 17.7 kg. By one year, males average 25.7 kg, while females weigh about 23.3 kg. Fully grown adult males can weigh between 24-65 kg, with an average of 42.8 kg, whereas females weigh between 16-50 kg, averaging 31.8 kg.

Hassan sheep have a lambing rate of approximately 64%, with an average lambing interval of 12 months. They are primarily managed under semi-intensive grazing systems, with many shepherds providing closed, kutcha-type sheds for housing. Sheep are grazed throughout the year, typically migrating within a 50 km radius. Common health management practices include regular deworming and vaccinations.

Under intensive feeding conditions, Hassan sheep lambs can grow at a rate of around 100 grams per day. For optimal meat yield, they are typically slaughtered between 12-15 months of age.

Physical Characteristics

  • Size: Small to medium
  • Color: Predominantly white with light brown or black spots
  • Ears: Medium-long and drooping
  • Horns: Males (~39%) may have horns; females are typically hornless
  • Tail: Short and thin
  • Fleece: Extremely coarse and open; belly and legs usually lack wool
